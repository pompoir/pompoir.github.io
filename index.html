Make Your Bed

When you're out walking the dog every day, you get a feeling for where you're allowed to bring pets in. Local hardware stores have a tendency to be dog-friendly, so I've slowly gravitated towards doing more of my shopping there. This led an impromptu purchase of a circular saw, which required a project to justify the purchase.

I decided to embrace adulthood and get my mattress up off the floor. Poking around YouTube led me to a design that seemed manageable, with the creator hosting detailed design schematics on their Instructables page. I had to make a couple changes, adapting a King configuration down to a Queen and nesting the mattress into the bed rather than having it sit precariously on top. I couldn't figure out AutoCAD software quickly enough, so I threw together some hand-drawn plans to get a feel for what supplies were needed.

To get away from the sawdust, the dog and I headed down to the hardware store to grab the metal brackets that will support the middle of the mattress and the slatted boards that hold everything in place. No need for these to be fancy, they'll be hidden from view when the job is complete. In fact, no hardware at all is visible on the final build due to using pocket holes, which are best understood by looking at how we assemble the headboard. I want to show off an unobstructed view of that natural wood, but it's imperative that the headboard be sturdy. Pocket screws are a solution that work alongside a drill jig that makes precise guide holes to securely attach boards at extreme angles. This ensures the screws hit the strong core of the next board and form a secure, covert connection. I've also used this strategy to connect the outer shell of the bed, and even just a couple of screws along a six foot long board yields enough strength to stand on without sag. With the headboard and outer shell framed up, were on to the finishing touches of the blueprints. We'll construct the support structure that runs down the middle and use it to mount bracing boards secured to either side. This design is extremely robust and eliminates the need for a box spring, which keeps the whole thing low-profile. The bed needs a final varnish to lock in the wood's lustre and protect it against moisture, but I haven't settled on which product to go with yet. I'm also not looking forward to dragging the whole thing outside to air out the varnish fumes, so stay tuned for a final picture after I've thrown a couple coats on!
